# Jobs to Be Done

## Core Concept

Every product, feature, habit, or system is "hired" by a person to do a job. The job exists independently of the solution. People don't want a quarter-inch drill — they want a quarter-inch hole. But even that formulation is too shallow: they don't want a hole either. They want to hang a shelf, which makes their kitchen functional, which makes them feel competent and in control of their living space. The job has layers, and the deepest layer is almost always emotional.

The Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) framework reframes product and system design away from features and demographics and toward the underlying job the user is trying to accomplish. It asks not "who is the user?" or "what features do they want?" but "what job are they hiring this product to do?"

This reframing is transformative because it reveals that competition is not between similar products — it is between all solutions to the same job. A task management app doesn't compete only with other task management apps. It competes with sticky notes, calendar reminders, asking a partner to remind you, and the habit of keeping everything in your head. All of these are "hired" for the same job: keeping track of what needs to be done.

## Key Principles

### The Three Simultaneous Jobs

Every job has three dimensions that exist simultaneously. A solution that addresses only one dimension will be outcompeted by a solution that addresses all three:

**Functional Job: What task does this help complete?**

The functional job is the practical outcome the user is trying to achieve. It is the most obvious and most commonly addressed dimension. "I need to track my tasks." "I need to get from point A to point B." "I need to eat something for lunch." Functional jobs are concrete, measurable, and relatively easy to identify.

But the functional job is rarely the reason a user chooses one solution over another. Most competing solutions address the functional job adequately. The differentiation lives in the emotional and social dimensions.

**Emotional Job: How should the user feel?**

The emotional job is the internal state the user is trying to achieve or maintain. This is the dimension that actually drives most hiring decisions, though users rarely articulate it (and often aren't aware of it).

Examples of emotional jobs:
- "I want to feel in control of my life" (not just "track my tasks")
- "I want to feel safe during this trip" (not just "get from A to B")
- "I want to feel like I'm taking care of myself" (not just "eat lunch")
- "I want to feel competent and organized" (not just "manage my calendar")
- "I want to feel like a good parent" (not just "help with homework")

The key insight: **a product that does the functional job perfectly but makes the user feel overwhelmed, anxious, guilty, or incompetent is failing.** A task manager that stores tasks perfectly but makes you feel behind is losing the emotional job. A fitness app that tracks workouts precisely but makes you feel ashamed of rest days is losing the emotional job. The emotional job is the job that actually matters.

**Social Job: How does this affect relationships?**

The social job is the interpersonal dimension — how the user's choice affects their relationships, social standing, and identity as perceived by others.

Examples of social jobs:
- "I want to be seen as someone who has their life together" (task management)
- "I want to share this experience with my family" (travel)
- "I want to be the kind of person who eats well" (food)
- "I want my team to see me as organized" (project management)
- "I want my partner to know I'm making an effort" (self-improvement)

Social jobs are often invisible because people are reluctant to admit that external perception influences their choices. But it does — profoundly. The social job explains why people choose visible solutions over invisible ones, why they share certain actions on social media, and why status markers are so powerful in product design.

### The Hiring Moment

The "hiring moment" is when the user actively chooses a solution for their job. Understanding what triggers this moment — and what competing solutions get "fired" — reveals the true product value.

**What triggers hiring:**
- A specific context (time of day, location, emotional state, social situation)
- A specific struggle (the current solution is failing, creating anxiety, or absent)
- A specific desire (wanting to feel differently than one currently feels)

**What causes firing:**
- The solution fails the emotional job (makes the user feel worse)
- A better solution appears for the same job
- The job itself changes (the user's needs evolve)
- Friction exceeds the value of the job being done

The hiring/firing lens prevents sunk-cost thinking. Users don't care about your feature set — they care about whether you do the job better than the alternative. If sticky notes do the emotional job better (simple, low-pressure, no guilt mechanics) than a sophisticated task manager, the sticky notes win.

### Competing Against Non-Consumption

The most overlooked competitor is not another product — it is **doing nothing.** Many jobs go unhired because the available solutions are all unsatisfying. The user's current approach to the job is often to ignore it, cope with it, or work around it.

Understanding non-consumption reveals untapped opportunity:
- "What job is the user currently NOT hiring anything for?"
- "What friction prevents them from hiring a solution?"
- "What emotional barrier makes all current solutions unacceptable?"

A task manager's biggest competitor is not another task manager — it is the user's decision to just "keep things in my head" because every task manager they've tried made them feel overwhelmed.

## How It Works

**Step 1: Identify the job.** What is the user trying to accomplish? Start with the functional job and then probe deeper:
- What functional outcome do they need?
- How do they want to feel during and after?
- How does this affect their relationships or self-image?

**Step 2: Map the current solution landscape.** What is currently hired for this job? Include all alternatives — products, habits, workarounds, people, and non-consumption.

**Step 3: Identify the struggle.** Where is the current solution failing? Which dimension (functional, emotional, social) is most underserved?

**Step 4: Design for the underserved dimension.** If the functional job is well-served but the emotional job is failing, that is the design opportunity. Do not add more functional features to solve an emotional problem.

**Step 5: Prototype the hiring moment.** Design the specific context, trigger, and experience that would cause the user to hire your solution and fire the current one.

## Applied As

- **In product design**: Before building any feature, identify the three jobs it serves. If you can't articulate the emotional job, the feature is incomplete. Design the emotional experience first, then build the functional capability around it.

- **In habit design**: Every habit you're trying to build is a "hire." What functional, emotional, and social jobs is this habit serving? If the emotional job isn't clear (or is negative — guilt, obligation, fear), the habit will be fired.

- **In marketing**: Speak to the job, not the feature. "Track unlimited tasks with customizable views" speaks to the functional job. "Feel in control of your day in under 3 minutes" speaks to the emotional job. The second is almost always more effective.

- **In self-understanding**: When you find yourself consistently choosing a particular behavior (even one you dislike), ask: what job is this serving? What three dimensions is it addressing? Understanding the job helps you design a better solution.

- **In relationship dynamics**: People "hire" each other for emotional and social jobs. Understanding what emotional job a friendship, partnership, or mentorship serves helps maintain and strengthen it.

## Diagnosing Hiring and Firing Decisions

The JTBD framework becomes most powerful when applied to moments of transition — when users switch from one solution to another (or from non-consumption to a solution). These transitions reveal the forces acting on the user:

**Forces driving switching:**
- *Push of the current situation*: What's wrong with the current solution? What pain or frustration does it cause? "My current to-do app makes me feel overwhelmed every time I open it."
- *Pull of the new solution*: What does the new solution promise? What job does it do better? "This new app shows me just one task at a time."

**Forces opposing switching:**
- *Allegiance to the current solution*: Habits, sunk costs, familiarity. "I've used this app for two years. All my data is there."
- *Anxiety about the new solution*: Risk, uncertainty, learning curve. "What if the new app doesn't work either? What if I lose my data in the migration?"

For a switch to happen, the combined push + pull must exceed the combined allegiance + anxiety. This force diagram explains many product puzzles:

- Why do users complain about a product but continue using it? (Allegiance exceeds push.)
- Why do users express interest in a new product but never switch? (Anxiety exceeds pull.)
- Why do some users switch instantly while others agonize? (Different force balances.)

The force diagram also reveals the most efficient intervention point. If anxiety about the new solution is the main barrier, don't improve the product — improve the onboarding experience and offer guarantees. If allegiance to the old solution is the barrier, don't argue for switching — reduce switching cost (easy data migration, familiar interface patterns).

## Common Misapplications

**Stopping at the functional job.** "Users want to track tasks." That's the functional job. The emotional job might be "feel in control" or "reduce anxiety" or "prove to myself I'm productive." If you stop at the functional layer, you build features. If you reach the emotional layer, you build experiences.

**Confusing the job with the solution.** "The user's job is to use our app." No — the user's job exists independently of your solution. Your app is one possible hire for that job. Confusing the two leads to designing for your product rather than for the user.

**Ignoring the social job in individual tools.** Even tools used alone have a social dimension. A personal journal has a social job ("I want to be the kind of person who journals"). A solo meditation practice has a social job ("I want to be seen as calm and centered"). Ignoring this dimension misses a significant motivational lever.

**Treating jobs as static.** The same person has different jobs in different contexts. Monday morning's job is different from Friday afternoon's job. Stress-state's job is different from calm-state's job. Context is part of the job.

## Integration Points

- **Behavior Equation**: The functional job maps to motivation. The ability dimension addresses friction in completing the job. The trigger is what activates the hiring moment.
- **North Star Test**: Stage 4 (feel good) directly maps to the emotional job. If the North Star Test fails at Stage 4, the emotional job is unaddressed.
- **Habit Loop Design**: The reward in the habit loop should address the emotional job, not just the functional completion.
- **Loss Aversion**: Loss-averse mechanics serve a specific emotional job — maintaining investment, avoiding waste. But they fail when the emotional cost (anxiety) exceeds the emotional benefit (continuity).
- **Identity Reinforcement**: The social job is closely linked to identity. Reinforcing identity ("you're someone who...") serves the social job dimension.
- **Concept Formation**: Identifying the "real" job a product is hired for is an act of essentialization — stripping away surface features to find the essential function, emotion, and social role.
- **Variable Reward Schedules**: The emotional job of "feeling good after completing something" is sustained long-term by variable rewards. Fixed rewards habituate, and the emotional job stops being served.
- **Reversibility Classification**: Whether to hire or fire a solution is itself a decision that can be classified. Switching productivity tools is usually a two-way door. Switching core business platforms may be a one-way door.

## Summary

The Jobs to Be Done framework transforms product and habit thinking from feature-centric to job-centric. The most powerful shift is recognizing that the emotional job is the real job — functional capability is table stakes, but the emotional experience of using a product or performing a habit is what determines whether it gets hired again tomorrow. Design for how the user should feel, and the functional requirements will organize themselves around that emotional core.

Every time a product or behavior fails to retain engagement, the first diagnostic question should be: which of the three jobs is underserved? The answer is almost always the emotional one.
