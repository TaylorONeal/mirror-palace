# Behavior Equation

## Core Concept

Behavior does not happen because people "want" it to happen. Behavior occurs when — and only when — three conditions are met simultaneously: sufficient Motivation, sufficient Ability, and a well-timed Trigger. This is the behavior equation:

**Behavior = Motivation x Ability x Trigger**

The multiplicative relationship is critical. If any factor is zero, the behavior does not occur regardless of the other two. A person with high motivation and high ability will not act without a trigger. A person with a perfect trigger and high motivation will not act if the ability barrier is too high. And a person with a well-timed trigger and easy ability will not act without sufficient motivation.

This equation demolishes the most common assumption in behavior change: that people don't act because they don't want to badly enough. This "motivation-first" assumption leads to strategies that try to increase motivation (inspirational messages, guilt, consequences) while ignoring the other two factors. In practice, motivation is the least reliable and most expensive lever to pull. Reducing friction (ability) and designing triggers is almost always more effective than trying to increase motivation.

The equation also explains why the same person acts in some contexts and not others. It is not that their character changes — it is that the configuration of motivation, ability, and trigger changes across contexts.

## Key Principles

### Motivation Levers

Motivation is the desire to perform the behavior. It is volatile — it fluctuates with mood, energy, context, and time of day. Because of this volatility, motivation should be supported but never relied upon as the sole driver.

**Progress visibility**: The most sustainable motivation lever. When people can see that their actions are producing results, motivation renews itself. This is not about celebrating completion — it is about making the *trajectory* visible. "You've completed 3 of 7 steps" is more motivating than "Good job finishing step 3!" because it shows movement toward a destination.

**Loss aversion**: The fear of losing what you've built. Powerful but dangerous — must be applied with care (see the Loss Aversion framework). Effective when used to protect genuine investment, destructive when used to manufacture guilt.

**Social proof**: The awareness that others are doing the same thing. "12 people in your group completed this today" provides motivation without pressure. Social proof works because it normalizes the behavior — it shifts it from "something I should do" to "something people like me do."

**Identity alignment**: Connecting the behavior to who the person believes they are. "You said you value health" is more motivating than "Exercise is good for you" because it ties the action to self-concept rather than abstract benefit.

**Intrinsic rewards**: The behavior itself is enjoyable or satisfying. This is the gold standard of motivation but cannot be manufactured. It emerges from engagement with activities that match skill level to challenge level, provide immediate feedback, and allow for autonomy.

### Ability Factors (Friction)

Ability is the ease of performing the behavior. It is the factor most within the designer's control and most commonly neglected. The core principle: **make the desired behavior easier, not more appealing.**

The six ability factors — each a source of friction that can prevent behavior:

**Time**: How long does the action take? Any action under 30 seconds is in the "trivially easy" zone. Actions over 5 minutes enter the "I'll do it later" zone. Between 30 seconds and 5 minutes is the "I'll do it if I'm motivated" zone. Design for the first zone whenever possible.

**Cost**: What financial or resource expenditure is required? Even trivial costs create friction. Free trials work not because people are cheap but because the cost friction is zero. A $1 barrier eliminates more behavior than a $100 barrier stops (the difference between "no friction" and "any friction" is larger than the difference between "some friction" and "more friction").

**Physical effort**: What physical action is required? Typing is harder than tapping. Tapping is harder than looking. Standing up to retrieve a device is harder than reaching for one in your pocket. Each physical step is friction.

**Cognitive load**: How much thinking is required? Choosing from 3 options is easy. Choosing from 20 is paralyzing. Filling in a blank field requires more cognition than selecting from a list. Open-ended prompts ("how do you feel?") require more cognition than structured prompts ("rate your energy 1-5").

**Social deviance**: Does this behavior go against what others around me are doing? Behaviors that require social deviance (working out in an office, meditating in a shared space, using an unusual tool) face higher friction than behaviors that are socially normal or invisible.

**Non-routine**: Does this behavior deviate from my current routine? New behaviors that fit into existing routines face less friction than behaviors that require building a new routine from scratch. Attaching a new habit to an existing anchor (habit stacking) reduces non-routine friction.

### Trigger Types

A trigger is the prompt that tells the person "do this now." Without a trigger, even highly motivated, highly able people will not act — because the behavior simply doesn't occur to them at the right moment.

**External triggers**: Push notifications, alarms, reminders, visual cues, other people. External triggers are the most reliable for new behaviors because they don't depend on internal states. They are best for early habit formation but should not be the permanent solution — the goal is to transition from external to internal triggers over time.

Critical rule: **never rely on the user remembering.** If a behavior depends on the user spontaneously remembering to do it, the trigger design has failed. Always prompt.

**Internal triggers**: Emotional states, physical sensations, recurring thoughts. Internal triggers are the most sustainable because they are self-generating. "When I feel anxious, I journal." "When I feel restless, I exercise." The emotional state itself becomes the cue. Internal triggers are the hallmark of established habits.

**Contextual triggers**: Time of day, location, preceding activity. Contextual triggers are the most reliable because they are regular and predictable. "After my morning coffee, I review my tasks." "When I arrive at the office, I check my calendar." Context provides a natural trigger without requiring memory or emotional state.

### The Interaction Effect

The equation is multiplicative, which means factors compensate for each other — but only up to a point:

- **High motivation compensates for moderate friction**: A person who desperately wants something will tolerate moderate difficulty. But even high motivation cannot overcome extreme friction (too many steps, too much time, too much confusion).
- **Low friction compensates for moderate motivation**: A person who is mildly interested will act if the action is trivially easy. This is why reducing friction is often more effective than increasing motivation — it makes the behavior possible even at low-motivation moments.
- **Good triggers compensate for moderate motivation**: A well-timed prompt catches the person at a moment when they're willing to act. This is why trigger design is so important — it's about finding the right moment, not the right message.

The strategic implication: **design for the minimum-motivation case.** Assume the user's motivation will be low (because some days it will be). Design the ability to be high and the trigger to be well-timed. This creates a system that works even on bad days.

## How It Works

**Step 1: Define the target behavior.** Be specific. Not "exercise more" but "do a 10-minute bodyweight workout." Not "be more organized" but "review tomorrow's calendar each evening."

**Step 2: Assess motivation.** What is driving this behavior? Is the motivation intrinsic or extrinsic? Is it reliable or volatile? Which motivation levers are active?

**Step 3: Assess ability.** Walk through the six friction factors. Which one is the biggest barrier? Where can friction be reduced?

**Step 4: Design the trigger.** What trigger will prompt the behavior at the right moment? Start with external triggers, plan for transition to contextual or internal triggers.

**Step 5: Find the weakest link.** Which of the three factors is most likely to fail? That is where to focus design effort.

**Step 6: Test and iterate.** Run the behavior for a week. Did it happen? If not, diagnose which factor failed. Adjust and retest.

## Applied As

- **In product design**: Every user action should be analyzed through the equation. Is the motivation sufficient? Is the friction minimal? Is the trigger present? If a feature has low adoption, diagnose which factor is failing before adding more features.

- **In habit design**: Build habits by starting with the ability factor — make the desired behavior ridiculously easy. Then design the trigger. Only address motivation if the first two are in place and the behavior still doesn't happen.

- **In system design**: Any workflow, process, or routine can be analyzed through the equation. Meeting attendance is low? Check: is the trigger clear (calendar invitation), is the ability present (right time, right tool, right room), is the motivation there (relevant agenda)?

- **In self-understanding**: When you fail to do something you intended to do, don't default to "I lack discipline." Ask: was the trigger present? Was the ability sufficient? Was the motivation aligned? Usually, the trigger was missing or the friction was too high.

- **In communication**: Every ask you make of another person is subject to the equation. Are they motivated to do what you're asking? Is it easy enough? Are you triggering the ask at the right moment?

## The Diagnostic Power: Why Didn't This Behavior Happen?

The behavior equation's greatest practical value is not in designing new behaviors but in diagnosing why existing intended behaviors aren't happening. When someone says "I keep meaning to do X but I never do," the equation provides a systematic diagnostic:

**Check the trigger first.** In the majority of cases, the trigger is the failing factor. The person had no prompt at the right moment. They relied on memory, and memory failed. The diagnostic question: "At the moment when you should have done X, what prompted you?" If the answer is "nothing — I just forgot," the problem is trigger design, not motivation or ability.

**Check ability second.** If the trigger was present but the behavior still didn't happen, friction is likely the cause. The diagnostic question: "When the moment came, what stopped you?" Common answers: "I didn't have enough time," "I couldn't find the thing I needed," "I didn't know what to do first," "It felt like too much." Each of these maps to a specific friction factor that can be reduced.

**Check motivation last.** Only if the trigger was present AND ability was sufficient should you investigate motivation. The diagnostic question: "You were prompted, and it was easy — why didn't you do it?" If the answer is genuinely "I didn't want to," then motivation is the issue. But this is rarer than most people assume. The vast majority of behavior failures are trigger failures or ability failures masquerading as motivation failures.

This diagnostic ordering — trigger, ability, motivation — is counterintuitive. People almost always self-diagnose behavior failure as a motivation problem ("I'm lazy," "I don't have discipline," "I don't want it badly enough"). But the equation reveals that most of what we call "laziness" or "lack of discipline" is actually a design problem: missing triggers, excessive friction, or both.

**The reframe that matters**: Replacing "I lack discipline" with "my system lacks triggers and has too much friction" is not just more accurate — it is actionable. Discipline is a character trait you can't redesign. Triggers and friction are design variables you can change today.

## Common Misapplications

**Motivation-first thinking.** The most common error. "People just need to want it more." Motivation is the least reliable factor. Design for low-motivation states instead.

**Ignoring triggers entirely.** A well-designed system with no triggers is like a well-built car with no ignition. The behavior doesn't start. Never assume people will remember.

**Reducing friction on the wrong behavior.** Make sure you're reducing friction on the *desired* behavior, not on an adjacent one. Making it easy to open an app is not the same as making it easy to complete the core action within the app.

**Treating the equation as additive.** The relationship is multiplicative, not additive. You cannot compensate for zero motivation with extreme ease. You cannot compensate for zero ability with extreme motivation (for long). All three must be present.

**Designing beautiful triggers for the wrong behavior.** Sending a perfectly-timed, beautifully-crafted notification that prompts the user to open an app is useless if the desired behavior is completing a task *within* the app. The trigger must prompt the target behavior, not an adjacent one. A notification that says "Time to review your tasks" triggers the target behavior. A notification that says "You have 12 unread items" triggers anxiety, not action.

## The Equation Applied to Asking Others

Every request you make of another person is subject to the behavior equation. Understanding this transforms communication:

**Motivation**: Does the other person have a reason to comply? Not your reason — their reason. If you are asking a colleague to review a document, their motivation might be: reciprocity (you've reviewed their work), professional obligation, genuine interest in the topic, or desire for relationship maintenance. If none of these are active, the request will fail regardless of how easy you make it.

**Ability**: Is it easy for them to comply right now? A request for a document review is easy if the document is attached, the deadline is clear, and the expected depth of review is specified. The same request is hard if they have to search for the document, guess the timeline, and figure out what level of feedback you want.

**Trigger**: Are you asking at the right moment? A request made in a busy Slack channel during a sprint is triggered at the wrong moment. The same request made after a standup where the topic was discussed is triggered at the right moment.

Most failed requests fail because the asker assumed motivation and didn't design for ability or trigger timing. Making requests easy to act on and timing them well is more effective than making the case for why the request matters.

## Integration Points

- **Habit Loop Design**: The behavior equation explains *whether* a behavior will occur. The habit loop explains *how* it becomes automatic. The trigger in the equation maps to the cue in the habit loop.
- **Jobs to Be Done**: Motivation in the equation is driven by the job the user is trying to accomplish — especially the emotional job.
- **North Star Test**: Stage 1 (know what to do) relates to trigger clarity. Stage 2 (feel confident) relates to motivation. Stage 3 (do it) relates to ability.
- **Variable Reward Schedules**: Variable rewards maintain motivation across repeated instances of the behavior.
- **Loss Aversion**: Loss aversion is a motivation lever — but only effective when ability and trigger are already in place.
- **Mirror Cognitive Architecture**: The Goals thread tracks motivation. The Talker layer must consider ability and trigger timing when delivering output.

## Summary

Behavior = Motivation x Ability x Trigger. All three must be present simultaneously. The multiplicative relationship means that zero in any factor means zero behavior, regardless of the other two. The most common error is assuming motivation is the problem ("they just don't want it enough") when the actual problem is a missing trigger or excessive friction. Design for the minimum-motivation case: assume the user's motivation will be low, and make the behavior work anyway through high ability and well-timed triggers. When diagnosing behavior failure, check trigger first, ability second, motivation last. Most of what we call "laziness" is a design problem.

The reframe that unlocks behavior change: stop asking 'why don't I want this enough?' and start asking 'what is missing from my system?' The answer is almost always a trigger or friction reduction, not more willpower.

The equation is diagnostic, not prescriptive. It tells you what is missing, not what to want.
