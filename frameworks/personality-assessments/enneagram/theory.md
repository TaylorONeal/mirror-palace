# Enneagram

## Core Concept

The Enneagram is a personality system built on motivation, not behavior. While the Big Five describes what you do (traits) and MBTI describes how you think (cognitive functions), the Enneagram describes why you do what you do -- the core fear, core desire, and defensive strategy that organize your personality from the inside. Two people can exhibit identical behavior for completely different reasons, and the Enneagram distinguishes between them by tracing the behavior back to its motivational root.

The system describes nine personality types, each organized around a specific core fear and a corresponding core desire. The types are not boxes -- they are centers of gravity. You have access to all nine patterns, but one is dominant: it is the lens through which you filter experience, the strategy you default to under stress, and the wound you organize your life around avoiding.

The Enneagram adds two additional layers of complexity: wings (adjacent types that flavor the primary type) and integration/disintegration lines (arrows that describe how each type shifts under growth and stress). Together, these create a dynamic map of personality that captures not just who you are at baseline but who you become when things go well and who you become when things fall apart.

## The Nine Types

### Type 1: The Reformer
**Core fear**: Being corrupt, defective, or wrong.
**Core desire**: To be good, to have integrity, to be correct.
**Strategy**: Maintain rigid internal standards and correct everything (self and world) that falls short. The inner critic is the loudest of any type -- an internal judge that monitors every thought, action, and impulse.
**At their best**: Principled, ethical, fair, wise, and deeply committed to improvement.
**At their worst**: Rigid, judgmental, self-righteous, resentful (anger suppressed as "inappropriate").

### Type 2: The Helper
**Core fear**: Being unwanted or unworthy of love.
**Core desire**: To be loved, needed, and appreciated.
**Strategy**: Earn love by being indispensable. Attend to others' needs, often at the expense of their own. The Two's generosity is genuine but carries an unconscious expectation of reciprocity.
**At their best**: Genuinely caring, empathetic, generous, and supportive without strings attached.
**At their worst**: Manipulative through helpfulness, martyred, possessive, unable to acknowledge own needs.

### Type 3: The Achiever
**Core fear**: Being worthless or without inherent value.
**Core desire**: To be valuable, successful, and admired.
**Strategy**: Become what the environment rewards. Adapt presentation to achieve success and earn validation. The Three is the most image-conscious type -- identity is constructed from achievement.
**At their best**: Effective, inspiring, authentic, and genuinely accomplished.
**At their worst**: Deceptive (including self-deception), vain, competitive, and disconnected from their real feelings.

### Type 4: The Individualist
**Core fear**: Having no identity or personal significance.
**Core desire**: To find themselves, to be unique, to be authentic.
**Strategy**: Cultivate a rich inner world and a distinctive identity. Focus on what is missing and on emotional depth. The Four feels fundamentally different from others and both suffers from and values this difference.
**At their best**: Creative, emotionally honest, self-aware, and deeply empathetic.
**At their worst**: Self-absorbed, melancholic, envious, and withdrawn into fantasy.

### Type 5: The Investigator
**Core fear**: Being helpless, incompetent, or overwhelmed.
**Core desire**: To be capable, competent, and to understand.
**Strategy**: Withdraw from demands and accumulate knowledge and resources. Minimize needs to reduce dependence. The Five conserves energy by disengaging from the world and observing from a safe distance.
**At their best**: Visionary, perceptive, innovative, and genuinely wise.
**At their worst**: Isolated, hoarding, detached, and arrogant about their knowledge.

### Type 6: The Loyalist
**Core fear**: Being without support, guidance, or security.
**Core desire**: To have security, certainty, and support.
**Strategy**: Anticipate danger, test loyalty, and seek (or become) reliable authority. The Six's relationship with authority is complex -- they may be obedient (phobic Six) or rebellious (counterphobic Six), but both orientations are organized around the question of authority.
**At their best**: Loyal, courageous, responsible, and genuinely trustworthy.
**At their worst**: Anxious, paranoid, indecisive, and reactive.

### Type 7: The Enthusiast
**Core fear**: Being trapped in pain, deprivation, or boredom.
**Core desire**: To be happy, satisfied, and to have options.
**Strategy**: Stay in positive territory by generating options, plans, and experiences. Reframe negatives as positives. Avoid pain by moving toward pleasure. The Seven's busyness is a defense against confronting what hurts.
**At their best**: Joyful, versatile, grateful, and genuinely present.
**At their worst**: Scattered, escapist, impulsive, and unable to tolerate discomfort.

### Type 8: The Challenger
**Core fear**: Being controlled, harmed, or violated.
**Core desire**: To protect themselves, to be strong, to control their environment.
**Strategy**: Take charge, assert power, and prevent vulnerability. The Eight builds a fortress of strength around a core of tenderness that they believe would be exploited if exposed.
**At their best**: Protective, decisive, magnanimous, and deeply caring.
**At their worst**: Domineering, confrontational, intimidating, and destructive.

### Type 9: The Peacemaker
**Core fear**: Loss of connection, fragmentation, or conflict.
**Core desire**: To have inner peace, harmony, and wholeness.
**Strategy**: Merge with others' agendas, minimize personal desires, and avoid conflict. The Nine achieves "peace" by numbing out, going along, and refusing to prioritize their own needs.
**At their best**: Accepting, patient, genuinely peaceful, and able to see all perspectives.
**At their worst**: Passive, disengaged, stubborn through inaction, and asleep to their own desires.

## Wings

Each type is influenced by one or both of its adjacent types on the Enneagram circle. A person whose primary type is 4 may have a 3-wing (4w3) or a 5-wing (4w5), and these wings significantly alter the expression of the primary type.

- **4w3**: More ambitious, image-aware, and outwardly creative. Combines emotional depth with achievement drive.
- **4w5**: More withdrawn, cerebral, and iconoclastic. Combines emotional depth with intellectual analysis.

The wing does not replace the primary type; it flavors it. Most people have a dominant wing, though some report relatively equal influence from both.

## Integration and Disintegration Lines

Each type has two arrows: one pointing to the type it moves toward in growth (integration) and one pointing to the type it moves toward in stress (disintegration). These movements are not about becoming another type -- they are about accessing the resources or the pitfalls of another type's pattern.

| Type | Stress (Disintegration) | Growth (Integration) |
|------|------------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Moves to 4 (moody, irrational) | Moves to 7 (spontaneous, joyful) |
| 2 | Moves to 8 (aggressive, controlling) | Moves to 4 (self-aware, authentic) |
| 3 | Moves to 9 (disengaged, numb) | Moves to 6 (loyal, committed to others) |
| 4 | Moves to 2 (clingy, over-involved) | Moves to 1 (principled, disciplined) |
| 5 | Moves to 7 (scattered, escapist) | Moves to 8 (decisive, action-oriented) |
| 6 | Moves to 3 (image-focused, competitive) | Moves to 9 (calm, trusting) |
| 7 | Moves to 1 (critical, perfectionistic) | Moves to 5 (focused, deep) |
| 8 | Moves to 5 (withdrawn, secretive) | Moves to 2 (caring, open-hearted) |
| 9 | Moves to 6 (anxious, reactive) | Moves to 3 (energized, effective) |

Understanding your stress arrow helps you recognize when you are deteriorating. Understanding your growth arrow shows you the direction of development.

## Centers of Intelligence

The nine types are organized into three centers:

**Body Center (8, 9, 1)**: Organized around anger and autonomy. The core question is: "How do I maintain my position in the world?"
**Heart Center (2, 3, 4)**: Organized around shame and identity. The core question is: "How do I create and maintain a valued self-image?"
**Head Center (5, 6, 7)**: Organized around fear and security. The core question is: "How do I feel safe?"

Each center has one type that externalizes the emotion (8-anger, 2-shame, 7-fear), one that internalizes it (1-anger, 4-shame, 6-fear), and one that is numb to it (9-anger, 3-shame, 5-fear).

## Relational Dynamics

Each type has characteristic patterns in relationships:
- Types that merge (2, 4, 9) -- risk losing themselves in others
- Types that withdraw (1, 5, 7) -- risk disconnecting when emotions arise
- Types that assert (3, 6, 8) -- risk dominating the relational space

Understanding your type's relational tendency helps you recognize when the pattern is running the relationship versus when you are genuinely connecting.

## Common Misapplications

- Using type as a fixed identity ("I'm a 7, so I can't do commitment")
- Typing others without their consent or interest
- Treating any type as better or worse
- Ignoring wings and arrows (the basic type alone is incomplete)
- Using the Enneagram to avoid change ("That's just my type")
- Over-identifying with average or unhealthy descriptions rather than aspiring toward growth

## Integration Points

This framework connects to:
- **Big Five** -- provides the trait dimension that the Enneagram's motivational focus does not capture
- **MBTI** -- provides cognitive style detail that complements the Enneagram's emotional focus
- **Four-F survival types** -- survival responses interact with type structure (a Type 6 in fight mode looks different from a Type 2 in fight mode)
- **Five wounds** -- significant overlap between wound/mask patterns and Enneagram type structures
- **Childhood emotional neglect** -- CEN impacts how each type's core fear develops
- **Family systems differentiation** -- type patterns interact with family system positions
