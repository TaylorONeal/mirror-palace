# The Five Wounds

## Core Concept

The five wounds model proposes that there are five fundamental emotional injuries that form during childhood, each producing a characteristic defensive mask -- a set of behaviors, body patterns, and relationship strategies designed to prevent re-experiencing the original wound. The wounds are: Rejection, Abandonment, Humiliation, Betrayal, and Injustice. Each wound has a corresponding mask, a characteristic body pattern, a relational signature, and a healing direction.

The model operates on a simple but powerful premise: your deepest pain is the architect of your personality. The things you do compulsively -- the patterns you cannot seem to break, the relationships you keep choosing, the reactions that feel automatic -- are not random. They are organized around avoiding the re-activation of a core wound. Understanding which wound is primary changes the question from "What is wrong with me?" to "What am I protecting?"

Every person carries all five wounds to some degree, but typically one or two are dominant. The dominant wound shapes the primary mask, which becomes so habitual that it feels like identity. "I am just a controlling person" may actually mean "I was betrayed and now I control everything to prevent it from happening again." The mask is not the self. The mask is the strategy the self developed to survive.

## The Five Wounds and Their Masks

### 1. Rejection -- The Withdrawal Mask

**The wound**: The experience of being rejected for who you are -- not for what you did, but for your essential being. The rejecting message is: "You should not exist" or "You are not wanted." This wound typically forms earliest, often in the first year of life or even in utero, when the child senses that their existence is unwelcome.

**The mask (Withdrawal)**: The person who carries the rejection wound retreats. They become invisible, take up as little space as possible, and develop an internal world as a refuge from an external world that does not want them. They are often highly intellectual, creative, and introspective -- not because these are inherent traits, but because the inner world is the only place that felt safe.

**Characteristic patterns**:
- Feeling fundamentally unwanted or like an outsider
- Retreating into isolation when stressed
- Difficulty believing they have a right to exist, take up space, or have needs
- Perfectionism as a way to earn the right to be (if I am perfect, maybe I will be wanted)
- Dissociation or detachment from the physical body
- Panic in social situations, not from shyness but from the deep conviction that they do not belong
- Relationships where they preemptively withdraw before the other person can reject them

**Body pattern**: The withdrawal mask often manifests as a contracted, small body or a body that seems to be trying to disappear. Narrow shoulders, collapsed posture, thin frame, a quality of not being fully "in" the body. The energy is pulled inward and upward, away from grounding.

**Healing direction**: Learning to take up space. Developing a felt sense of belonging. Moving from "I must earn the right to exist" to "I exist, and that is enough." Physical practices that increase body awareness and grounding are particularly important for this wound.

### 2. Abandonment -- The Dependence Mask

**The wound**: The experience of being left -- emotionally or physically -- by a caregiver the child depended on. The abandoning message is: "You are not important enough for me to stay." Unlike rejection (which says "you should not exist"), abandonment says "you can exist, but I will not be there for you." The wound typically forms between ages one and three.

**The mask (Dependence)**: The person who carries the abandonment wound clings. They develop an intense need for connection, attention, and reassurance. They may appear needy, dramatic, or emotionally intense -- not because they are attention-seeking in a superficial sense, but because they are trying to prevent the devastating experience of being left again.

**Characteristic patterns**:
- Difficulty being alone
- Intense fear of being left or forgotten
- Drama or emotional escalation as a bid for connection
- Attaching quickly and intensely in relationships
- Difficulty ending relationships even when they are clearly unhealthy
- Helplessness as an unconscious strategy (if I cannot take care of myself, you must stay)
- Victim identity: the world is consistently doing things to them
- Emotional ups and downs that track relational proximity (close = euphoric, distant = despairing)

**Body pattern**: The dependence mask often manifests as a body that seems to reach outward. Soft, underdeveloped musculature. Long limbs. A quality of leaning toward others. The body may appear younger than chronological age. Energy flows toward others rather than being contained within.

**Healing direction**: Developing self-sufficiency without counter-dependence. Learning that being alone is not the same as being abandoned. Building an internal source of comfort and stability that does not require another person to activate. The goal is not to stop needing people but to need them without being consumed by the fear of losing them.

### 3. Humiliation -- The Masochism Mask

**The wound**: The experience of being shamed for physical needs, pleasure, or the body itself. The humiliating message is: "Your needs are disgusting" or "You should be ashamed of yourself." This wound typically forms between ages one and three, often around experiences of bodily autonomy -- eating, toilet training, physical exploration, sensory pleasure.

**The mask (Masochism)**: The person who carries the humiliation wound punishes themselves before anyone else can. They take on suffering, self-sacrifice, and self-denial as preemptive strategies: if I am already suffering, the world cannot shame me further. They may become the caretaker, the one who always gives, the one who puts themselves last -- not from generosity but from the conviction that their own needs are shameful.

**Characteristic patterns**:
- Chronic self-sacrifice and over-giving
- Difficulty receiving pleasure without guilt
- Shame around the body, appetite, or physical needs
- Taking on others' burdens as if they were their own
- Difficulty setting limits on how much they give
- Using humor -- particularly self-deprecating humor -- to manage social situations
- Compensatory behaviors: overeating, overworking, over-giving
- A deep conviction that wanting things for themselves is selfish

**Body pattern**: The masochism mask often manifests as a thick, heavy body -- the body itself becomes a container for the shame, adding layers of protection. The body may carry excess weight particularly around the midsection. There is a quality of being weighed down, earthbound. Movements may be slow and heavy.

**Healing direction**: Learning that their needs are not shameful. Developing the capacity to receive without guilt. Establishing limits on giving. Reclaiming pleasure as a birthright, not a transgression. The specific challenge is that the masochism mask is often socially rewarded (self-sacrifice is praised), making it harder to recognize as a wound pattern.

### 4. Betrayal -- The Control Mask

**The wound**: The experience of having trust violated by a caregiver -- usually the opposite-sex parent. The betraying message is: "I will promise and not deliver" or "I will use your trust against you." The wound typically forms between ages two and four, when the child is developing trust and the capacity for relationship.

**The mask (Control)**: The person who carries the betrayal wound becomes controlling. They develop strength, charisma, and the ability to manage situations and people -- not because they are naturally dominant, but because control is the only defense against the unpredictability that devastated them. If they control everything, no one can betray them.

**Characteristic patterns**:
- Need to control situations, outcomes, and other people
- Difficulty delegating or trusting others to handle things
- Strong, charismatic presence that often attracts followers
- Anger or contempt when expectations are not met
- Speed and intensity as default operating modes
- Difficulty with vulnerability or showing weakness
- Seduction as a power strategy (emotional, intellectual, or physical)
- Intolerance of ambiguity -- everything must be certain and managed
- Trust issues that manifest as monitoring, testing, or loyalty demands

**Body pattern**: The control mask often manifests as a powerful, imposing body. Broad shoulders, strong musculature, a commanding physical presence. The body projects strength and dominance. The energy is directed outward, filling the space, demanding attention. Upper body may be notably more developed than lower body.

**Healing direction**: Learning to trust without controlling. Developing tolerance for vulnerability and uncertainty. Releasing the need to manage every outcome. The specific challenge is that the control mask is often effective -- the person genuinely is competent and capable -- so the mask does not feel like a mask. It feels like strength. Recognizing that the compulsive quality of the control is the wound, not the competence itself.

### 5. Injustice -- The Rigidity Mask

**The wound**: The experience of being treated unfairly, particularly by the same-sex parent. The unjust message is: "You are valued for what you do, not who you are" or "Perfection is the price of love." The wound typically forms between ages four and six, when the child is developing a sense of fairness and social rules.

**The mask (Rigidity)**: The person who carries the injustice wound becomes rigid. They develop exacting standards, precise self-control, and a deep commitment to fairness and correctness. They are often admired for their discipline and integrity. But the rigidity is not virtue -- it is armor. The conviction is: if I am perfectly fair, perfectly correct, perfectly controlled, then I cannot be treated unjustly.

**Characteristic patterns**:
- Perfectionism rooted in the belief that anything less is unacceptable
- Difficulty with spontaneity, play, or letting go
- Strong sense of justice and fairness that can become rigid moralism
- Cross when others do not follow the rules
- Emotional suppression: feelings are controlled or denied
- Difficulty asking for help (asking is admitting imperfection)
- Workload management through sheer willpower
- Self-criticism that is harsh, precise, and relentless
- A tendency to justify emotions rather than simply feel them

**Body pattern**: The rigidity mask often manifests as a straight, taut, well-proportioned body. Good posture, controlled movements, contained energy. The body is held, managed, organized. Jaw clenching and locked joints are common. The body reflects the personality: everything in its place.

**Healing direction**: Learning to relax control without catastrophizing. Allowing imperfection. Developing access to spontaneous feeling. Letting the body soften and move freely. The specific challenge is that the rigidity mask is socially valued (discipline, fairness, reliability), so the person receives positive reinforcement for the very pattern that keeps them trapped.

## Wound Activation and Interaction

The wounds interact in predictable ways:
- Stress reactivates the primary wound, which triggers the mask
- Intimate relationships activate wounds most intensely because they mirror the original caregiver dynamics
- Two people's wounds often interlock: the abandonment wound seeks the person who will eventually withdraw (rejection wound), confirming both wounds simultaneously
- The mask that protects against one wound often inflicts another wound on others (the control mask betrays trust by being manipulative; the withdrawal mask abandons by disappearing)

## Identifying Your Primary Wound

The primary wound is identified not by what happened to you (the event) but by what you are most afraid of (the fear) and what you do to prevent it (the mask):

| Wound | Core Fear | Mask Behavior |
|-------|-----------|--------------|
| Rejection | Being told you should not exist | Withdrawal, invisibility, isolation |
| Abandonment | Being left, being alone | Dependence, clinging, drama |
| Humiliation | Being shamed for your needs | Self-sacrifice, over-giving, self-denial |
| Betrayal | Being deceived or powerless | Control, dominance, distrust |
| Injustice | Being treated unfairly | Rigidity, perfectionism, emotional control |

## Healing Principles

1. **Recognize the mask as a mask**: The first step is seeing that the pattern is not identity. "I am controlling" becomes "I developed control as a response to betrayal."

2. **Feel the wound underneath**: The mask exists to prevent feeling the wound. Healing requires gradually allowing the original pain to surface in a safe context.

3. **Grieve what was lost**: Each wound represents something that should have been present in childhood and was not: acceptance (rejection), presence (abandonment), respect (humiliation), trustworthiness (betrayal), fairness (injustice).

4. **Practice the opposite of the mask**: Withdrawal practices taking up space. Dependence practices solitude. Masochism practices receiving. Control practices letting go. Rigidity practices spontaneity. Small, repeated practices are more effective than dramatic gestures.

5. **Accept the wound as permanent and manageable**: The wound does not disappear. It becomes less reactive, less dominant, less automatic. The mask softens from compulsion to choice. Full healing is not the absence of the wound but the capacity to feel it without being controlled by it.

## Common Misapplications

- Using the wounds to label and categorize others rather than to understand yourself
- Treating the body patterns as diagnostic criteria rather than as tendencies
- Assuming one-to-one correspondence between wound and a specific parental behavior
- Competing over whose wound is worst
- Using wound identification to avoid responsibility for current behavior
- Conflating the mask with the person (the mask is a strategy, not an identity)

## Integration Points

This framework connects to:
- **Four-F survival types** -- survival responses often organize around the primary wound
- **Emotionally immature parents** -- the parent type maps loosely to the wound created
- **Embodied awareness** -- the body patterns require somatic attention, not just cognitive understanding
- **Subconscious repatterning** -- the masks operate below conscious awareness and require body-level work
- **Big Five personality assessment** -- wound patterns influence trait expression
- **Enneagram** -- significant overlap between wound/mask patterns and enneagram type structures
