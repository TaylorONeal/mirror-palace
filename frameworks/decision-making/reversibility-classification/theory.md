# Reversibility Classification

## Core Concept

Every decision falls into one of two categories based on a single characteristic: whether or not you can undo it. This binary classification — one-way doors versus two-way doors — is the most efficient first filter for determining how much deliberation a decision deserves.

A **one-way door** (Type 1 decision) is irreversible or nearly irreversible. Once you walk through, the door closes behind you. The cost of reversal is so high — in time, money, reputation, relationships, or opportunity — that the decision is effectively permanent. These decisions deserve careful analysis, deliberation, consultation, and the full weight of your critical thinking.

A **two-way door** (Type 2 decision) is reversible. You can walk through, look around, and walk back if you don't like what you see. The cost of reversal is low. These decisions should be made quickly, by individuals with good judgment, not by committees or extended deliberation. Most decisions are two-way doors.

The insight that makes this framework transformative is not the classification itself — it is the identification of the dominant failure mode. **The primary failure is treating two-way doors like one-way doors**: over-analyzing reversible decisions, waiting for perfect information on low-stakes choices, building consensus for decisions that one person should just make. This failure mode is far more common and far more costly than its opposite.

## Key Principles

### The Asymmetry of Error

The two types of misclassification have different costs:

**Treating a two-way door as a one-way door (common, costly):**
- Over-analysis leads to delay
- Delay has compounding costs — missed opportunities, lost momentum, decision fatigue
- Perfect information is never available; waiting for it is wasted time
- Creates a culture of risk aversion where nothing moves quickly
- The decision often becomes harder the longer you wait, not easier

**Treating a one-way door as a two-way door (rare, dangerous):**
- Moving fast on irreversible commitments without sufficient analysis
- Signing contracts without understanding terms
- Making public statements that cannot be retracted
- Ending relationships or leaving positions without adequate reflection
- Committing resources that cannot be recovered

Both errors are real, but the first is epidemic. Most people, most organizations, and most planning processes are biased toward over-deliberation. The corrective is to make the reversibility check the *first* step in any decision process, and to give yourself explicit permission to move fast on two-way doors.

### How to Classify

The classification question is not "how important is this?" or "how scary does this feel?" It is specifically: **"If this turns out to be wrong, can I reverse it at acceptable cost?"**

**One-way door indicators:**
- The commitment is contractual or legal
- Other people will make irreversible plans based on your decision
- The decision involves permanent resource expenditure (selling an asset, spending savings)
- Public reputation is at stake in a way that cannot be undone (publishing, public statements)
- Relationship damage would be permanent or very difficult to repair
- The opportunity cost is real — choosing this path forecloses others

**Two-way door indicators:**
- The decision can be changed tomorrow, next week, or next month
- The cost of reversal is lower than the cost of delay
- You can run a small experiment to test the decision before fully committing
- No one else's irreversible plans depend on your choice
- The worst-case outcome is manageable and temporary

**Gray area indicators (treat as one-way with a fast deadline):**
- Partially reversible — you can undo it, but at significant cost
- Reversible in theory but not in practice (you could quit, but the social pressure makes it unlikely)
- Reversible for you but not for others affected by the decision

### The Speed Principle

For two-way doors, **speed of decision is itself a value.** Every hour spent deliberating on a reversible decision is an hour not spent learning from the result of that decision. The information you gain by acting is almost always more valuable than the information you gain by analyzing.

This does not mean being careless. It means:
- Gathering the minimum information needed for a reasonable judgment (not the maximum)
- Setting a short time-box for the decision (minutes or hours, not days or weeks)
- Accepting that you might be wrong and planning for that possibility
- Biasing toward action over analysis for anything that can be undone

For one-way doors, the opposite applies: **thoroughness of analysis is itself a value.** Take the time. Consult people whose judgment you trust. Examine worst-case scenarios. Sleep on it. The cost of a week's delay on a truly irreversible decision is almost always less than the cost of getting it wrong.

### Decision Velocity as a Competitive Advantage

In any system — personal, organizational, or creative — the rate at which decisions are made and learned from is a primary determinant of progress. Decision velocity is not the same as recklessness. It is the result of correctly classifying decisions and applying the appropriate level of deliberation to each.

High-performing individuals and organizations do not make better decisions on any single choice. They make decisions faster on the vast majority of choices (which are two-way doors), freeing up cognitive and temporal resources for the few decisions that truly require depth (one-way doors).

The bottleneck in most systems is not decision quality. It is decision *throughput*.

## How It Works

**Step 1: State the decision clearly.** What are you actually choosing between? Vaguely framed decisions cannot be classified.

**Step 2: Ask the reversibility question.** "If this turns out to be wrong, can I reverse it at acceptable cost?" Be specific about what "reverse" means and what "acceptable cost" means.

**Step 3: Classify.**
- If clearly reversible at acceptable cost: Two-way door. Proceed to Step 4A.
- If clearly irreversible or reversible only at unacceptable cost: One-way door. Proceed to Step 4B.
- If ambiguous: Treat as one-way door but set a fast deadline for the decision.

**Step 4A (Two-way door): Decide now.** Gather the minimum necessary information. Make the call. Set a review point to evaluate the decision later. Move on.

**Step 4B (One-way door): Deliberate properly.** Define what information you need. Consult trusted perspectives. Examine worst-case scenarios. Set a deadline for the decision (even one-way doors shouldn't take forever). Then decide.

**Step 5: Review.** For two-way doors, check back at the review point. Was the decision correct? If not, reverse it. For one-way doors, check whether the decision played out as expected. Update your mental models either way.

## Applied As

- **In daily decisions**: Most of what you agonize over is a two-way door. "Should I go to that event?" "Should I try this new tool?" "Should I reorganize my task list?" Just do it. You can undo it. Stop deliberating.

- **In career decisions**: Distinguish between truly irreversible career moves (relocating internationally, accepting equity-only compensation) and seemingly-big-but-actually-reversible moves (trying a new role, taking on a project, starting a side project). Most career decisions are more reversible than they feel.

- **In relationship decisions**: Some are genuine one-way doors (ending a marriage, having a child). Most daily relationship decisions are two-way doors (having a difficult conversation, setting a boundary, suggesting a change). Treat the second category accordingly.

- **In system design**: Build systems that make decisions more reversible. Feature flags that allow rollback. Modular architecture that allows replacement. Contracts with exit clauses. The goal is to convert one-way doors into two-way doors wherever possible.

- **In organizational leadership**: Create explicit decision classification processes. Empower individuals to make two-way door decisions without consensus. Reserve committee deliberation for one-way doors only.

## Converting One-Way Doors to Two-Way Doors

One of the most powerful applications of reversibility thinking is not classifying existing decisions but *redesigning decisions to be more reversible.* In many cases, what appears to be a one-way door can be converted into a two-way door through structural changes:

**Staging**: Instead of committing fully, commit to a stage. Don't quit your job to start a business — negotiate a sabbatical, start evenings and weekends, or take on a part-time arrangement. Each stage provides information that makes the next stage lower-risk.

**Piloting**: Run a small experiment before committing fully. Don't redesign the entire product — ship a feature flag to 5% of users. Don't move to a new city permanently — rent for six months. Pilots convert one-way doors into two-way doors by limiting the commitment scope.

**Contracting exit clauses**: When signing agreements, negotiate reversibility. Break clauses in leases, trial periods in employment, refund windows in purchases. The cost of negotiating these exits is almost always worth the optionality they preserve.

**Modular architecture**: In systems design (both technical and organizational), modularity converts large one-way decisions into collections of small two-way decisions. Monolithic commitments are one-way doors. Modular commitments are two-way doors because individual modules can be replaced without replacing the whole.

**Information purchases**: Sometimes the most efficient path through a one-way door is to spend a small amount of time or money to acquire information that changes the classification. "Should I change careers?" feels like a one-way door. "Should I spend $200 and a weekend at a career exploration workshop?" is clearly a two-way door — and it may provide the information that makes the larger decision feel clearer.

The meta-principle: before deliberating on a one-way door, ask whether you can convert it to a two-way door. Often you can, and the conversion costs less than the deliberation.

## Common Misapplications

**Confusing magnitude with reversibility.** A decision can be large (big budget, many people affected) and still be a two-way door. And a decision can be small (one conversation) and be a one-way door (if it permanently damages trust). Size and reversibility are independent dimensions.

**Using "I'm not sure" as an excuse to treat everything as one-way.** Uncertainty about the *outcome* does not make a decision one-way. The question is whether you can *undo the choice*, not whether you can *predict the result*.

**Ignoring the cost of delay.** When you fail to decide on a two-way door, you are implicitly deciding to wait — and that waiting has real costs. Indecision is itself a decision, and usually a bad one for reversible choices.

**Assuming one-way means "never decide."** One-way doors still need decisions. They just need better-informed ones. Set a deadline for one-way door decisions too — unlimited deliberation is not thoroughness, it is avoidance.

**Classifying based on emotional weight rather than actual reversibility.** "Should I have a difficult conversation with my partner?" feels heavy and scary. But it is almost always a two-way door — you can have the conversation, and if it goes poorly, you can have another one to clarify, repair, or recalibrate. The emotional weight makes it *feel* like a one-way door. But feeling irreversible and being irreversible are different things. Always classify based on the actual structure of the decision (can I undo it?), not the emotional texture (does it scare me?).

**Failing to set review dates for two-way doors.** The value of a two-way door is that you can walk back through it. But this only works if you actually check whether the decision was correct. A two-way door decision without a review date is effectively a one-way door — you walked through and never looked back. Set a review date for every two-way door decision: one week, one month, one quarter. At the review, ask: is this working? If not, reverse it.

## Integration Points

- **Regret Minimization**: For one-way doors, the regret minimization framework provides the evaluation method. Project forward and ask: will I regret not doing this?
- **Information Compression**: One-way door decisions require retaining the full reasoning chain in compressed context. Two-way door decisions can be compressed to just the decision and review date.
- **Concept Formation**: Classifying a decision as one-way or two-way is an act of essentialization — identifying the essential characteristic (reversibility) and omitting incidental measurements (magnitude, complexity, emotional weight).
- **North Star Test**: For two-way doors in product/feature decisions, apply the north star test to evaluate the decision quickly.
- **Behavior Equation**: Two-way doors with high friction (low ability) still won't get made fast. Reduce friction on reversible decisions.

## Summary

Reversibility classification is the most efficient first filter for any decision. Ask one question: "If this turns out to be wrong, can I reverse it at acceptable cost?" If yes, decide quickly and review later. If no, deliberate carefully with a deadline. The dominant failure mode — treating reversible decisions as irreversible — costs more in aggregate than all bad irreversible decisions combined. Speed on two-way doors. Thoroughness on one-way doors. And always look for ways to convert one-way doors into two-way doors.

The framework is simple. The discipline of applying it is the hard part.
