# Structured Self-Coaching

## Core Concept

Most self-reflection is unstructured rumination. It loops without landing. The same thoughts circulate, the same feelings arise, and the same conclusions are reached -- or worse, no conclusions are reached at all. Structured self-coaching is the antidote: a deliberate, time-bound, written protocol that converts emotional experience into behavioral commitment. It is not therapy. It is not journaling. It is a 30-minute weekly discipline that bridges the gap between insight and action.

The critical distinction is between thinking about a problem and working on a problem. Thinking is private, fluid, and often circular. Working is structured, externalized, and directed. When you write, you think differently than when you ruminate. Writing forces linearity, specificity, and commitment. A thought can be vague; a sentence cannot. A feeling can be contradictory; a written commitment must be concrete. The medium changes the process.

The protocol is deliberately simple because complexity is the enemy of consistency. The goal is not a perfect self-analysis but a reliable practice that accumulates insight over time. Thirty minutes, once per week, following a fixed structure. The power is in the repetition, not in any single session.

## The Weekly Protocol

### Time and Setting
- 30 minutes, same time each week
- Written, not typed (handwriting engages different neural pathways and slows thinking to a productive pace)
- Private, uninterrupted
- Not optional -- treat it like a meeting with someone whose opinion matters to you

### The Five Questions

The protocol consists of five questions asked in sequence. Each builds on the previous one. The order matters.

#### Question 1: What happened?

This is a factual inventory of the week's significant events. Not an emotional narrative -- a list. What actually occurred? Meetings, conversations, decisions, conflicts, achievements, surprises, disappointments. Be specific and behavioral. Not "work was stressful" but "I had three deadline conflicts, a difficult conversation with a colleague about project scope, and missed one workout."

The purpose of starting with facts is to ground the reflection in reality rather than in the emotional story about reality. Facts first, feelings second. This sequence prevents emotional reasoning from hijacking the process.

#### Question 2: What did I feel?

Now bring in the emotional layer. For each significant event identified in Question 1, what did you feel? Not what you thought about it, not what you should have felt, but what you actually felt. Use feeling words, not thinking words. "I felt anxious" not "I felt like things were out of control." "I felt angry" not "I felt that they were being unfair."

This question is where most people struggle, particularly those with alexithymia or emotional neglect backgrounds. If you draw a blank, try the body: Where did you feel tension? Heaviness? Heat? Constriction? Physical sensations are doorways to emotional identification.

The felt truth principle operates here: the feeling is information, not instruction. You do not have to act on a feeling, but you do have to acknowledge it. Unacknowledged feelings drive behavior from the shadows. Acknowledged feelings can be evaluated and integrated.

#### Question 3: What pattern is this?

This is the analytic move. Step back from the specific events and feelings and look for the recurring structure. Have you felt this before? Have you been in this situation before? Is this a new experience or a familiar groove?

Pattern recognition is the highest-leverage self-coaching skill. A single instance of anxiety is a feeling. A recurring pattern of anxiety in a specific context is information about your operating system. Patterns to look for:
- Repeated emotional responses to similar situations
- Recurring relationship dynamics
- Consistent avoidance behaviors
- Cyclical motivation patterns
- The same mistake in different clothing

Naming the pattern reduces its power. An unnamed pattern runs you. A named pattern is something you can observe, evaluate, and choose to respond to differently.

#### Question 4: What will I do differently?

This is the behavioral commitment. Based on what you have observed in Questions 1-3, what specific, concrete, observable behavior will you commit to in the coming week? Not a feeling you will try to have. Not an attitude you will adopt. A behavior.

Requirements for a good behavioral commitment:
- Specific: "I will have the scope conversation with my colleague by Thursday" not "I will be more assertive"
- Observable: someone watching you could confirm whether you did it
- Time-bound: when will you do it?
- One to three commitments maximum -- more than that and you commit to nothing
- Connected to the pattern: the behavior should interrupt or counter the pattern identified in Question 3

#### Question 5: What is the felt truth?

This is the closing move and the most subtle. The felt truth is the statement that captures what this week's reflection is really about -- the underlying reality that the events, feelings, and patterns point to. It is often uncomfortable. It is always honest.

Examples:
- "I am avoiding the conversation because I am afraid of being rejected."
- "I keep saying yes because I do not believe I am valuable enough to say no."
- "I am exhausted because I am running from something, not toward something."

The felt truth is not a solution. It is a diagnosis. It is the honest statement that, once externalized, changes the game. You cannot unsee a felt truth once you have written it down. And that is the point.

## Why Written, Not Just Thought

Writing is non-negotiable in this protocol. The reasons:

1. **Externalization**: Writing takes the thought out of the loop and puts it in a form that can be examined. You cannot examine a thought while you are still having it.

2. **Specificity**: Writing requires you to choose words. Choosing words forces precision. "I feel bad" in your head becomes "I feel ashamed that I did not follow through" on paper. The second version is actionable; the first is not.

3. **Accountability**: A written commitment is harder to rationalize away than a mental one. The paper remembers what the mind conveniently forgets.

4. **Accumulation**: Over weeks and months, the written record becomes a dataset. Patterns that are invisible in a single week become obvious across twenty. The self-coaching protocol is a longitudinal study of one person.

5. **Neural engagement**: Handwriting activates different cognitive processes than typing. It slows the thinker down, engages motor memory, and creates a qualitatively different relationship with the content.

## The Felt Truth Principle

The felt truth is the concept that anchors this entire protocol. A felt truth is different from an intellectual insight in that it lands in the body, not just the mind. You can have hundreds of intellectual insights about your patterns and change nothing. A single felt truth can reorganize your behavior because it connects knowing with feeling.

Characteristics of a felt truth:
- It feels slightly uncomfortable to admit
- It is specific to your situation, not a generality
- It usually involves something you are avoiding or denying
- It often begins with "I" and contains a verb
- It does not require anyone else to do anything
- It survives the "midnight test": if you woke up at 3 AM and read it, you would know it was true

The felt truth is not a mantra or an affirmation. It is an honest observation that, because it is honest, creates the conditions for change. Change does not come from deciding to change. It comes from seeing clearly what is actually happening. The felt truth is the act of seeing clearly.

## Behavioral Commitment Design

The behavioral commitment is where self-coaching becomes coaching rather than just self-reflection. The commitment must be:

### Connected to the pattern
If the pattern is avoidance, the commitment involves approaching. If the pattern is over-commitment, the commitment involves saying no. If the pattern is emotional editing, the commitment involves saying one honest thing.

### Appropriately sized
Too large and it becomes another source of failure. Too small and it does not challenge the pattern. The right size is: uncomfortable but achievable. You should feel a slight resistance to the commitment, not paralysis.

### Reviewed the following week
Question 1 of the next week's protocol should include: Did I follow through on last week's commitment? If yes, what happened? If no, what got in the way? This closes the loop and prevents the protocol from becoming a weekly aspiration exercise with no accountability.

## Common Failure Modes of Self-Coaching

1. **Skipping the feelings**: Jumping from Question 1 to Question 4, from events to action, without passing through feelings and patterns. This produces behavioral changes that do not address the underlying dynamic.

2. **Staying in feelings**: Spending the entire 30 minutes on Question 2 and never reaching commitment. This converts the protocol into a venting exercise.

3. **Vague commitments**: "I will try to be more present." This is not a commitment. It is a wish.

4. **Too many commitments**: Five behavioral changes in a week is a recipe for zero behavioral changes.

5. **Skipping weeks**: The protocol works through accumulation. One session is a thought exercise. Twenty sessions is a transformation practice. Consistency is the mechanism.

6. **Typing instead of writing**: Typing is faster, more comfortable, and less effective. The discomfort of handwriting is a feature, not a bug.

7. **Sharing too early**: The self-coaching protocol is private. Sharing it prematurely converts honest self-examination into performance. Wait until the insight is solid before externalizing it to others.

## Integration with Other Frameworks

The five questions naturally surface content for other frameworks:
- Question 2 (feelings) connects to **embodied awareness** and **childhood emotional neglect** (difficulty identifying feelings)
- Question 3 (patterns) connects to **failure modes**, **four-F survival types**, and **five wounds** (recurring defensive patterns)
- Question 4 (commitments) connects to **stories vs. facts** (ensuring commitments are based on reality, not narrative)
- Question 5 (felt truth) connects to **subconscious repatterning** (body-level acknowledgment of what is true)

## Common Misapplications

- Treating the protocol as therapy (it is a maintenance practice, not crisis intervention)
- Using it to plan rather than to reflect (planning is a different cognitive mode)
- Performing the protocol for an imagined audience rather than for honest self-examination
- Replacing the behavioral commitment with an emotional intention ("I will feel more confident")
- Using the protocol to beat yourself up rather than to see clearly

## Integration Points

This framework connects to:
- **Stories vs. facts** -- the protocol distinguishes what happened from the story about what happened
- **Failure modes** -- many failure modes become visible through consistent self-coaching
- **Embodied awareness** -- the felt truth requires body-level awareness
- **Developmental stages** -- the protocol can be adapted for different developmental needs
- **Psychological contracts** -- hidden contracts often surface in Question 3 (pattern recognition)
