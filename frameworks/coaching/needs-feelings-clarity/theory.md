# Needs & Feelings Clarity

## Core Concept
People often react from a blurred mix of sensation, story, interpretation, and demand. This framework helps separate those layers so an experience can be understood and acted on more cleanly.

The key move is simple: do not confuse a feeling with a judgment, and do not confuse a need with a preferred tactic. A person can be full of real pain and still describe it in a distorted way. The goal is not to police language. The goal is to recover usable signal.

This framework is especially useful when someone says things like "I feel ignored," "I feel trapped," or "I feel misunderstood." Those phrases may point to something real, but they often contain mind-reading or accusation rather than naming a clean emotional state. The deeper questions are usually: what actually happened, what was felt, what seems threatened or missing, and what action would actually serve the situation?

## Key Principles

### Principle 1
Needs are broad human requirements or conditions for flourishing. Strategies are specific ways someone hopes to meet those needs.

### Principle 2
Feelings are signals, not verdicts. They do not automatically tell the full truth of the situation, but they reveal that something meaningful is touched.

### Principle 3
Pseudo-feelings or faux feelings often hide accusation inside emotional language. Translating them creates clarity.

### Principle 4
Precision matters, but endless labeling does not. If the model increases clarity but decreases action, it is being misused.

## How It Works
The framework inserts a sequence between reaction and action:
1. What happened factually?
2. What do I actually feel if I remove blame-language?
3. What need or value seems touched?
4. What am I calling a need that is actually a tactic?
5. What action would serve the deeper need?

Useful need domains often include:
- physical regulation
- safety and steadiness
- connection and belonging
- autonomy and choice
- recognition and mattering
- meaning and direction
- growth and competence
- play and aliveness
- integrity and alignment

Useful emotional signal families often include:
- activation / threat
- loss / grief
- frustration / boundary pressure
- disconnection / loneliness
- shame / diminishment
- fulfillment / joy
- curiosity / orientation

## Applied As

- **In daily decisions**: clarifying whether a reaction is about safety, agency, recognition, or something else before choosing a response
- **In system design**: creating check-ins or journaling prompts that reduce accusation and improve signal quality
- **In self-understanding**: separating what hurts from the story about why it hurts
- **In relationships**: distinguishing real longing from protest behavior and real need from preferred reassurance tactics

## Common Misapplications
- Using needs-language to avoid a clear boundary or exit
- Treating every feeling as unquestionable truth
- Over-intellectualizing emotions instead of acting on obvious reality
- Confusing external compliance with deeper need satisfaction
- Mistaking a specific person, role, or circumstance for the need itself

## Integration Points
This framework pairs especially well with:
- **Stories vs Facts** when narrative is contaminating emotional signal
- **Structured Self-Coaching** when someone needs a repeatable reflection sequence
- **Awareness as Intervention** when naming the real need is enough to reduce reactivity
- **Family Systems Differentiation** when emotional language is entangled with relational roles and inherited patterns

It should often come before more interpretive frameworks, because it cleans the signal those frameworks depend on.
