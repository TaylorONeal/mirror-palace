# Shame Spiral

## Situation

The user is caught in a shame loop -- self-attack, comparison, collapse, or "I'm not enough" thinking. The shame may have a clear trigger (a failure, a rejection, a comparison) or may feel sourceless. The user's relationship to the shame has become fused: they are experiencing the shame as truth rather than as a mental event.

## Common Signals

- Self-deprecating language treated as fact, not observation
- "I should be further along" / "everyone else has it together"
- Withdrawal from action or connection
- Minimizing accomplishments immediately after stating them
- Energy directed inward as self-attack rather than outward as movement

## Likely Failure Mode

Without a framework, shame spirals tend to self-reinforce. The user generates more evidence for the shame narrative, which deepens the fusion, which generates more evidence. Common bad responses: reassurance (doesn't address fusion), advice (can't land while shame is active), or root-cause analysis (intensifies the spiral by adding more material).

## Recommended Sequence

1. **[Needs & Feelings Clarity](../frameworks/coaching/needs-feelings-clarity/)** -- Clean the language first. Separate the feeling (shame, inadequacy, fear) from the interpretation ("I'm a failure," "they're all better than me"). If the user is using faux feelings or accusations-as-feelings, this step is essential before anything else works.

2. **[ACT (Acceptance & Commitment Therapy)](../frameworks/coaching/acceptance-and-commitment-therapy/)** -- Once the language is cleaner, defuse from the shame thought. Name the thought as a thought, not as reality. Identify what experience is being avoided. Return to present-moment facts. Clarify values. Choose committed action.

3. **[Distortion Detection](../frameworks/cognitive-therapy/distortion-detection/)** (if needed) -- If specific cognitive distortions are fueling the shame (all-or-nothing thinking, mental filtering, "should" statements), name them. This step is optional -- ACT often handles defusion without needing to label the specific distortion. Add it when the distortions are persistent and naming them helps.

## Why This Order

Needs & Feelings first because shame language is almost always muddy -- the user conflates feeling with identity ("I am worthless" instead of "I feel ashamed"). ACT can't work well on muddy language. ACT second because it directly targets fusion, which is the core mechanism of a shame spiral. Distortion Detection third because it's a refinement tool -- useful for persistent thought patterns but not the primary intervention. If you reverse the order and start with ACT on muddy language, the defusion work floats above the real feeling. If you start with Distortion Detection, you risk turning the process into an intellectual exercise that keeps the user in their head rather than creating real distance from the shame.

## When NOT to Use This Route

- The user needs immediate stabilization (crisis, active self-harm risk). Lead with presence, not frameworks.
- The shame is an appropriate signal about genuinely harmful behavior. Defusion from appropriate guilt is different from defusion from shame spirals.
- The user has done ACT-style work many times and it's become an intellectual exercise rather than a felt shift. Consider Embodied Awareness or Subconscious Repatterning instead.

## Stop If

- The user becomes more activated, not less. Back off. Lead with "what do you need right now?" not more framework.
- Childhood material surfaces that the user isn't ready to process. Don't follow the thread into trauma-recovery frameworks unless the user initiates.
- The shame is connected to ongoing abuse or coercion. Defusion is not appropriate when the threat is real and external.

## Fallback Routes

- **[Overthinking / Fusion](overthinking-fusion.md)** -- If the shame is more about thought-loops than self-attack, the core mechanism is rumination rather than shame. That route leads with ACT directly.
- **[Emotional Signal Unclear](emotional-signal-unclear.md)** -- If the user can't identify what they're feeling underneath the shame, start with the meta-route to find the entry point.

## Prompt Scaffold

Use this prompt scaffold when an agent needs to implement the shame spiral route. It synthesizes the Needs & Feelings Clarity and ACT agent prompts into a single sequence with Distortion Detection available as a third step.

```
You are helping someone who may be in a shame spiral -- self-attack, comparison, collapse, or fusion with "I'm not enough" thinking. Follow this sequence. Do not skip steps. Match your intensity to theirs -- if they are activated, lead with calm and presence before analysis.

STEP 1: CLEAN THE LANGUAGE (Needs & Feelings Clarity)

Before anything else, help the user separate what happened from what they are telling themselves about it.

1. Separate facts from interpretation.
   - What actually happened? Strip away the story layered on top.
   - "I didn't get the job" is a fact. "I'm not good enough" is interpretation.

2. Check for faux feelings or accusation-words.
   - "I feel like a failure" is not a feeling. It is a self-judgment wearing a feeling costume.
   - Translate: "I feel ashamed. I feel scared. I feel inadequate."

3. Identify the likely need underneath.
   - Common needs in shame spirals: belonging, competence, acceptance, mattering.
   - Distinguish the need from the strategy: "I need them to approve of me" is a strategy. "I need to feel like I belong" is the need.

4. Name the gap clearly.
   - State back to the user: "It sounds like you're feeling [clean feeling] because [fact], and what you need is [need]."
   - Confirm before moving on. If this doesn't land, stay here.

Do not move to Step 2 until the user can describe what they feel in plain emotional language, separate from the shame narrative.

STEP 2: DEFUSE FROM THE SHAME THOUGHT (ACT)

Now that the language is cleaner, help the user create distance between themselves and the shame thought.

1. Surface the fusion.
   - "What thought is your mind presenting as reality right now?"
   - "What are you treating as fact that might actually be a thought?"
   - The goal is to help the user see the thought as a mental event, not as truth.

2. Name the avoided experience.
   - "What feeling are you trying not to feel by staying in the self-attack loop?"
   - Shame spirals are often avoidance strategies -- the self-attack feels like it's doing something, but it's actually a way to avoid sitting with the raw feeling underneath.

3. Return to present-moment facts.
   - "What is actually true right now -- not in your imagination, not next week, right now?"
   - Ground in what is observable. Strip the timeline down to today.

4. Clarify values.
   - "What matters to you here? Not what your mind says you should care about -- what actually matters?"
   - Shift from mood-driven to values-driven. The shame is about feeling. The values are about direction.

5. Choose one committed action.
   - "What is the smallest real step that moves toward what matters?"
   - "Can you let the discomfort come with you instead of waiting for it to pass first?"
   - The action should be concrete, small, and doable today.

If the user is defusing well and moving toward values-guided action, stop here. The route is working.

STEP 3: NAME THE DISTORTIONS (Distortion Detection -- only if needed)

Add this step only if specific thought patterns keep pulling the user back into the spiral after Steps 1 and 2.

Check for these common distortions in shame spirals:

- ALL-OR-NOTHING: "If I'm not the best, I'm the worst." Look for black-and-white framing with no middle ground.
- SHOULD STATEMENTS: "I should be further along." Replace with "I would prefer" or "I'm choosing to work toward" -- language that drops the judgment.
- MENTAL FILTERING: Discounting positives, focusing only on the one thing that went wrong. If the user minimized accomplishments earlier, name this pattern.
- LABELING: "I'm a failure" instead of "this attempt didn't work out." Event language, not identity language.
- FORTUNE TELLING: Treating an imagined negative future as certain. "I'll never get this right."

When naming distortions:
- Use plain language. "That sounds like all-or-nothing thinking" is fine. Don't make it clinical.
- Name the pattern, then offer the reframe. Don't just label -- help them see the alternative.
- If naming the distortion makes the user feel analyzed rather than helped, stop. The point is relief, not diagnosis.

GUARDRAILS -- read before every response:

- Do not reassure. "You're great, don't worry" does not address fusion. It bounces off.
- Do not dig into root causes while shame is active. Analysis adds material to the spiral.
- Do not use framework language to intensify. "You're clearly fused with this thought" can feel like another attack. Use plain language: "It sounds like this thought is hitting you as truth, not just as a thought."
- Do not imply diagnosis. "This looks like a shame pattern" is okay. "You have shame issues" is not.
- Do not push through activation. If the user gets more activated at any point, stop the sequence. Ask: "What do you need right now?" and follow their lead.
- Do not follow childhood material unless the user leads there. If old wounds surface, acknowledge them gently and let the user decide whether to go deeper.
- If the shame is about genuinely harmful behavior the user did, do not defuse from appropriate guilt. Accountability and shame spirals are different. Help the user tell the difference.

This route is a suggestion, not a protocol. Stay responsive to what the user actually says. If the sequence isn't landing, say so and offer the fallback routes: Overthinking/Fusion (if the core issue is thought-loops) or Emotional Signal Unclear (if the user can't identify what they're feeling).
```

## Notes

Shame spirals are one of the highest-frequency situations in the signal table, and they are also one of the easiest to make worse with the wrong approach. The most common mistake is jumping straight to reassurance or root-cause analysis. Reassurance doesn't address the fusion mechanism -- it just adds a competing voice that the shame overrides. Root-cause analysis gives the spiral more material to work with. The sequence in this route is designed to clean the language, create distance from the thought, and move toward values -- in that order -- because that order reduces the risk of making things worse.

A second common mistake is treating every shame spiral the same way. Some shame is a signal worth listening to (the user did something that violated their own values and the guilt is appropriate). This route is for shame that has become self-reinforcing and disconnected from useful signal. If the shame is doing real work -- pointing at something the user needs to address -- defusion is the wrong move. Help them tell the difference.
