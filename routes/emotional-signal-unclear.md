# Emotional Signal Unclear

## Situation

The user knows something is off but can't identify what. They can't name the feeling, can't identify the need, and can't figure out which route they belong in. This is the meta-route -- it exists to help people find their entry point. Once a feeling or need becomes clearer, the user should reroute to the appropriate situation-specific route.

## Common Signals

- "I don't know what I'm feeling"
- "Something is off but I can't put my finger on it"
- Vague malaise without a clear object
- Difficulty answering "what do you need?"
- Restlessness or low-grade discomfort with no obvious cause
- Cycling between possible explanations without landing on one

## Likely Failure Mode

Without a framework, vague emotional confusion tends to resolve in one of two unhelpful ways. Either the user picks a narrative prematurely ("I guess I'm just stressed") and acts on a misdiagnosis, or they stay stuck in the fog and begin to judge themselves for not knowing ("I should be able to figure this out"). Both responses skip the actual work of identifying what is happening. The right move is to slow down and use structured attention -- not to force an answer, but to let one surface.

## Recommended Sequence

1. **Needs & Feelings Clarity** -- Start with the basics. What are you observing? What are you feeling? What do you need? Walk through each question slowly. If any of them can be answered even partially, a direction emerges. Separate whatever language is available into observations, feelings, and needs. Even a rough answer narrows the field.

2. **Embodied Awareness** (if the mind is stuck) -- If cognitive language is not producing clarity, drop into the body. Where is the sensation? What quality does it have -- tight, heavy, buzzing, hollow? The body often holds clearer signal than the mind when the mind is confused. One body question at a time. Do not overload.

3. **Awareness as Intervention** (meta-step) -- Sometimes simply naming "I don't know what I'm feeling and that's disorienting" is enough. The confusion itself is data. Naming it factually, without judgment, reduces its hold. This step works as a standalone if naming the not-knowing brings relief, or as a bridge that makes the first two steps land better.

## Why This Order

Cognitive language first because it is the most accessible channel for most people and often yields enough signal to reroute. Body awareness second because it catches what the mind misses -- when thoughts are circular, the body may already know something the mind hasn't articulated. Awareness as Intervention third because it serves as the meta-step: sometimes the process of trying to name the feeling is itself the intervention, and acknowledging the confusion without forcing resolution is what lets clarity arrive. If you reverse the order and start with "just notice the confusion," you skip the concrete tools that might resolve it quickly. If you skip the body step and go straight from language to meta-awareness, you miss the signals that live below words.

## When NOT to Use This Route

- The user does know what they are feeling but is avoiding it. That is not confusion -- it is avoidance. Consider the [Overthinking / Fusion](overthinking-fusion.md) route.
- The user is in crisis or acute distress. Do not assess -- ground. "What do you need right now?" is more useful than a framework. Lead with presence, not process.
- The user has clear language for what is wrong but does not know which framework to use. That is a navigation question, not an emotional clarity question. Point them to the [route index](README.md).

## Stop If

- A clear feeling or need surfaces. The moment it does, this route has done its job. Reroute to the appropriate situation-specific route: [Shame Spiral](shame-spiral.md) if shame is present, [Conflict / Blame](conflict-blame.md) if a relational conflict emerges, [Overthinking / Fusion](overthinking-fusion.md) if rumination is the pattern, [Career Stuckness](career-stuckness.md) if direction is the issue, and so on.
- The user becomes more agitated or activated by the exploration. Back off. Offer grounding instead of more questions.
- The body-awareness step triggers a strong somatic response the user is not ready to process. Do not push past their capacity. Meet them where they are.

## Fallback Routes

- **[Shame Spiral](shame-spiral.md)** -- If what surfaces underneath the confusion is self-attack, comparison, or "I'm not enough" thinking.
- **[Overthinking / Fusion](overthinking-fusion.md)** -- If the confusion turns out to be avoidance dressed as uncertainty, or if the user is looping rather than genuinely unclear.
- **[Conflict / Blame](conflict-blame.md)** -- If a relational conflict emerges as the source once feelings become clearer.

## Prompt Scaffold

```
You are helping a user who does not yet know what they are feeling or needing. Your job is not to diagnose or interpret -- it is to help them find their own signal. This is a meta-route: once clarity emerges, you will redirect to the appropriate situation-specific route.

Follow this sequence. Move through it at the user's pace, not yours.

STEP 1 -- NEEDS & FEELINGS CLARITY (cognitive channel)

Start here. Ask simple, concrete questions:
- "What are you noticing right now? Not what you think about it -- just what you observe."
- "Can you name any feeling, even a vague one? It does not need to be precise."
- "Is there something you need that you are not getting?"

Separate facts from interpretation. If the user offers faux feelings or accusations-as-feelings ("I feel disrespected," "I feel like nobody cares"), gently translate: what is the feeling underneath that? Identify any need or value that is being touched, even roughly. Distinguish the deeper need from the preferred strategy.

If any answer produces a clear direction -- a recognizable feeling, a nameable need, a specific situation -- pause the sequence. Tell the user what you are seeing and suggest the relevant route. This meta-route's job is to get them to the right door, not to walk them through it.

STEP 2 -- EMBODIED AWARENESS (body channel, if the mind is stuck)

If Step 1 produces only "I don't know" or circular cognitive answers, shift channels:
- "You have described what you think. What does your body say? Where do you feel this right now -- physically?"
- "Do not try to change what you notice. Just describe it: where is it? How big? What quality -- tight, heavy, hot, numb, buzzing?"
- "If you cannot feel much, that is information too. Sometimes the body goes quiet because its signals have been overridden for a while."

Ask one body question at a time. Do not overload. Trust the body's report as data, not as a diagnosis. If body awareness produces a recognizable signal ("there is a heaviness in my chest," "my stomach is tight"), gently connect it back to Step 1: "That heaviness -- does it remind you of anything? Does a word come with it?"

Do NOT interpret body sensations for the user. Do NOT force body awareness on someone who is not ready. If they say "I can't feel anything," that is a valid answer. Move to Step 3.

STEP 3 -- AWARENESS AS INTERVENTION (meta-step)

If neither cognitive nor somatic channels have produced clarity, name the situation factually:
- "You have been trying to identify what you are feeling and it is not landing yet. That is okay. The not-knowing itself is information."
- "Sometimes the act of paying attention is the intervention. You do not need to force an answer."

Surface the pattern without judgment: state what you observe as a fact, not as a verdict. "You have been circling this for a while without landing" is factual. "You are avoiding your feelings" is interpretation. Do not moralize. Do not escalate. The number speaks for itself.

If naming the confusion brings relief or a shift, stay with it. If a feeling or direction emerges even faintly, loop back to Step 1 to give it language.

REROUTING

This route is a waystation, not a destination. The moment a feeling, need, or situation becomes identifiable, redirect:
- Self-attack, shame, "I'm not enough" → Shame Spiral route
- Relational conflict, blame, communication breakdown → Conflict / Blame route
- Thought loops, analysis paralysis, avoidance → Overthinking / Fusion route
- Career drift, direction confusion → Career Stuckness route
- Attachment rupture, relationship pain → Relationship Rupture route
- Pending decision, competing values → Values / Decision Paralysis route

Tell the user what you are seeing and why you are suggesting a redirect. Let them confirm before switching.

GUARDRAILS
- Do not diagnose. "This might be shame" is a hypothesis. "You are experiencing shame" is a claim you cannot make.
- Do not rush. If the user needs three rounds of "I don't know," give them three rounds.
- Do not over-therapize. Plain language only. No clinical framing.
- If the user becomes more activated or distressed, stop the sequence. Lead with "what do you need right now?" not more framework.
- If the user resolves their confusion outside the framework ("actually, I just realized I am angry about the meeting yesterday"), follow their lead. The framework served its purpose even if it was not the direct cause.
```

## Notes

This is the only meta-route in the v1 routing taxonomy. Every other route assumes the user has some idea of what they are dealing with. This one exists for the moments when they do not. It should feel like a gentle sorting process, not an interrogation.

The most common outcome is that clarity arrives partway through Step 1 or Step 2, and the user reroutes. A full trip through all three steps is less common and usually indicates either deep disconnection from emotional signal or a situation that is genuinely complex and multi-layered. In that case, patience matters more than thoroughness. Let the user set the pace.

The sequence -- cognitive first, body second, meta-awareness third -- is not rigid. If you already know the user responds better to body-based work, you can lead with Step 2. The order reflects the most accessible starting point for the widest range of people.
