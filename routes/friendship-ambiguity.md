# Friendship Ambiguity / Mixed Signals

## Situation

The user is confused about a friendship -- receiving mixed signals, unsure if the friendship is reciprocal, dealing with unclear boundaries, or processing a friendship that has changed without acknowledgment. This is not a romantic or deep attachment bond in crisis; it is the murkier, less-scripted territory of friendships where expectations were never made explicit and something feels off.

## Common Signals

- "I can't tell if they actually want to be friends"
- Inconsistent contact patterns (warm then distant)
- Effort imbalance that hasn't been discussed
- "Am I overreacting?" about a friendship concern
- Comparing the friendship to what they think friendship "should" be
- Interpreting silence or distance as a verdict on their worth

## Likely Failure Mode

Without a framework, friendship ambiguity tends to produce one of two loops. The user either over-interprets (builds a story about the friend's motives from sparse evidence and acts on the story) or under-acts (sits with the confusion indefinitely, hoping it resolves on its own while resentment builds). Both loops avoid the harder work of identifying what they actually need and whether the friendship, as it currently exists, meets that need.

## Recommended Sequence

1. **Needs & Feelings Clarity** -- Start here. The user often arrives with a tangle of feeling and interpretation fused together: "They don't care about me" is not a feeling, it is a conclusion. Separate the feeling (loneliness, hurt, confusion) from the interpretation. Then identify the specific unmet need. "I need more consistent contact" or "I need to feel like this friendship is mutual" are actionable. "They don't value me" is not.

2. **Psychological Contracts** -- Once the need is clearer, surface the invisible agreement. Friendships run on implicit contracts that are rarely spoken: "We check in weekly," "We show up for the hard stuff," "We are honest even when it is uncomfortable." When these contracts are violated without acknowledgment, it creates confusion and resentment that feels disproportionate to the specific event. Naming the contract makes it possible to decide whether to renegotiate, accept, or release.

3. **Stories vs Facts** (if needed) -- If the user has built a strong narrative about the friend's motives ("They are pulling away on purpose," "They only reach out when they need something"), separate observed behavior from interpretation. "They have not initiated plans in three months" is a fact. "They do not value me" is a story. The facts may support concern -- but the story is not the same as the facts, and acting on story rather than facts usually makes things worse.

4. **[NVC Translation](../frameworks/coaching/nvc-translation/)** (if the user is drafting an outgoing message) -- If, after the inward work, the user wants to actually reach out to the friend, the NVC Translation engine produces a mode-aware rewrite (self-expression, empathy guess, boundary, or clarity request). For friendship ambiguity, clarity-request mode is often the right choice -- a question that surfaces the missing observation or intent rather than an accusation framed as a feeling.

## Why This Order

Needs and feelings first because the user cannot evaluate the friendship clearly until they know what they actually need from it. Most friendship confusion is downstream of an unidentified need. Psychological Contracts second because once the need is clear, the next question is "did we ever agree to meet this need?" -- and the answer is usually no, which reframes the situation from betrayal to misalignment. Stories vs Facts third because narrative distortion tends to be strongest when the need and the contract are both unclear; once those are named, the story often loosens on its own. If it does not, this framework separates what happened from what the user decided it means.

## When NOT to Use This Route

- The friendship involves a close bond with attachment dynamics (lifelong best friend, someone the user depends on for emotional safety). Consider the [Relationship Rupture](relationship-rupture.md) route -- the bond may be deep enough that attachment frameworks apply.
- The "friendship" involves manipulation, pressure, or coercion. This route assumes two people with good intentions and unclear communication. If one party is using the friendship instrumentally or exerting control, consider Influence Defense frameworks instead.
- The friendship concern is actually about the user's broader loneliness or social anxiety, not about this specific person. The specific friendship may be a symptom, not the issue.
- The situation has become an active conflict with blame and accusation. Consider the [Conflict / Blame](conflict-blame.md) route.

## Stop If

- The user becomes more anxious or self-critical as they examine the friendship. Back off from analysis and lead with "what do you need right now?" The framework should reduce distress, not amplify it.
- The exploration reveals that the user is consistently the one making accommodations across many friendships, not just this one. That is a broader pattern (possibly a people-pleasing or self-worth pattern) that a single friendship route will not resolve.
- The user is using the framework to build a case against the friend rather than to understand their own needs. Redirect to their own experience.
- The friendship involves dynamics the user is not ready to look at (e.g., the friend reminds them of a family member, or the friendship serves a role the user has not acknowledged). Do not push into territory the user has not opened.

## Fallback Routes

- **[Relationship Rupture](relationship-rupture.md)** -- When the bond is deep enough to involve attachment dynamics, protest behavior, or pursue-withdraw cycles. If the user describes the friendship in terms that sound more like a close partnership, this route is likely a better fit.
- **[Conflict / Blame](conflict-blame.md)** -- When the friendship issue has escalated into active conflict with accusation-heavy language, faux feelings, or blame cycling. If the user is saying "they always" and "they never," the language needs cleaning before the friendship itself can be examined.

## Prompt Scaffold

```
You are helping a user navigate confusion about a friendship. Follow this sequence. Do not skip steps. Match your intensity to the user's capacity -- if they seem activated or distressed, lead with presence before analysis.

STEP 1: NEEDS & FEELINGS CLARITY

Apply the Needs & Feelings Clarity framework first.

- Separate facts from interpretation. What has actually happened (what a camera would record) versus what the user has concluded about it?
- Identify faux feelings or accusation-words if present. "I feel used" is an interpretation, not a feeling. Translate it: "I feel hurt and confused when I am the one reaching out every time."
- Identify the cleaner emotional states: loneliness, hurt, confusion, disappointment, fear of rejection.
- Name the underlying need: consistency, reciprocity, honesty, reassurance, belonging.
- Distinguish the need from the preferred strategy. "I need them to text me more" is a strategy. "I need to feel like this friendship is mutual" is the need.

Output to the user:
- The facts as you understand them
- The clean feelings you are hearing
- The likely need underneath
- Any strategy being confused for the need

Check in before moving on: "Does this feel accurate?"

STEP 2: PSYCHOLOGICAL CONTRACTS

Once the need is clearer, surface the invisible agreement.

- Listen for the contract beneath the confusion. When the user says "I always check in on them but they never check in on me," there is an unspoken contract: "I provide consistent attention; in return, I expect the same." Name it plainly: "It sounds like there is an unspoken agreement here -- 'I show up reliably, and in return, I expect you to show up reliably.' Was that ever discussed?"
- Validate the feeling before naming the structure. The hurt is real even if the contract was never spoken.
- Help distinguish between reasonable expectations and invisible contracts. Wanting reciprocity is healthy. The problem is when the expectation was never communicated, the friend never agreed to it, and the gap produces outsized distress.
- Guide toward action: make the contract explicit (have the conversation), adjust the terms (give only what you are willing to give freely without expecting return), or release (acknowledge the contract was never agreed to and grieve the gap).

Do NOT:
- Frame the user's expectations as unreasonable just because they were unspoken
- Diagnose contracts in the friend without care -- you only have one side
- Push the user to "just let it go" -- unspoken contracts often have nervous system encoding that requires processing, not willpower

STEP 3: STORIES VS FACTS (if needed)

Apply this step if the user has a strong narrative about the friend's motives.

- Reflect back the fact-story separation: "The facts are: they have not initiated contact in two months. The story you are adding is: they do not value the friendship. Those are different things."
- Offer the multiple-stories test: "Those facts could support several stories. Your story is that they are pulling away. Another story is that they are overwhelmed and bad at reaching out. What makes you certain it is the first one?"
- Guide toward action from facts, not from stories: "Setting the story aside -- given just the facts, what would you want to do?"

Do NOT:
- Use fact-checking to dismiss the user's feelings ("you are just telling yourself a story")
- Present the most charitable interpretation of the friend as the correct one -- you do not know
- Strip the story away faster than the user can tolerate

GUARDRAILS:
- If the user becomes more activated or self-critical, stop analysis and lead with presence.
- If a pattern emerges across many friendships (not just this one), name it gently but do not try to resolve it within this route.
- If the friendship involves manipulation or coercion, do not use this route. The frameworks here assume good faith on both sides.
- Do not tell the user what their friendship should look like. Help them see what it is and what they need, then let them decide.
- This is a navigational aid, not a diagnosis. Use language like "consider" and "it might be worth looking at," not "you are experiencing" or "this means."
```

## Notes

- Friendship ambiguity is under-served by most relational frameworks because the cultural scripts for friendship are weaker than those for romance or family. There is no "friendship therapy" equivalent to couples counseling. This means users often lack language for what they are experiencing, which makes the Needs & Feelings step especially important.
- The effort imbalance signal is the most common entry point. Users frequently describe doing more than the friend and feeling unappreciated. This almost always has a psychological contract underneath it.
- Be careful not to pathologize normal friendship drift. Friendships change, and not every change is a problem to solve. Sometimes the most useful outcome of this route is the user recognizing that the friendship has shifted and deciding how to respond to that shift, rather than trying to restore something that has organically moved on.
